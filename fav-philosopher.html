Albert Camus was a French-Algerian philosopher, author, and journalist. He was one of the most influential figures of the 20th century, best known for his development of the philosophy of Absurdism. In 1957, at the age of 44, he became the second-youngest recipient in history of the Nobel Prize in Literature.

Early Life

Camus was born in French Algeria to a poor family. His father died during World War I, and he was raised by his mother in harsh economic conditions. Despite this, his intellectual brilliance led him to study philosophy at the University of Algiers. His background in poverty deeply influenced his views on the human condition and injustice.

Philosophy of the Absurd

The core of Camus' work is the "Absurd"—the conflict between the human search for meaning and the "silent," meaningless universe. Unlike nihilists who believe life is not worth living, Camus argued that we should accept the absurd and continue to live defiantly.

"The struggle itself toward the heights is enough to fill a man's heart. One must imagine Sisyphus happy."
Personal Connection

I admire Camus because his philosophy doesn't offer easy answers; it offers a way to endure. His idea that we should find happiness and purpose despite the lack of inherent meaning in the world is something that resonates with my own worldview. He encourages us to rebel against the void by living as fully and authentically as possible.
