My Story

I am Dave Christopher, better known online as Dave Pham. I am a boy searching for the meaning of life and purpose. I am also an atheist, a person who does not believe in God. Sometimes it feels like this is not just a simple story about me, but a narrative of a life that seems to have no direction. If you happen to read this someday, I may already be dead, or I may still be alive. I do not know, because there are moments when I wish I could disappear.

This is my story—or perhaps my attempt to understand how a life is shaped.

I was born on July 19, 2010. I do not know exactly where I was born because I never asked my mother. The only clear memory I have is the place where we lived. As far as I remember, it was Masbate, though I am not entirely sure. We were happy then. Our family was complete. I had a female friend back then, but I cannot recall her name because it has been so long.

Regarding my father, I know very little about him. All I heard was that he was a bodyguard or the right-hand man of the captain in our town, but I am not certain. We moved to Bulacan when I was very young, maybe three or four years old. Life was okay even though my father was far away because he was working. It was usually just my mother and me at home.

I was probably already attending daycare. But even as a child, I could see the conflicts between my parents. They fought often. I heard that my father had another woman. Embarrassing as it is to say, that was the reason I heard. In the end, it led to their separation.

My mother took us to the province, to Burias Island, where my grandparents lived. I was probably five or six years old. My mother left us there because she needed to work in Manila for us. So my grandparents took care of me and my sibling.

A year or two passed. I was in Grade 1. My grandmother took care of us every day. Later, my mother returned and took us back. There were two of us siblings. I will not name my sibling. My sibling stayed with our aunt in Bulacan while I went with my mother to Quezon City to another aunt.

I am a mama’s boy. Wherever my mother is, I want to be there too. We stayed with my aunt while my mother worked. I was in Grade 2. I had friends in class, but I cannot remember their names. That was also when I first felt what is called a first crush, though I cannot recall their name. I was mischievous and somewhat disobedient back then.

Our family problems resurfaced. I do not know exactly what happened, but I remember we were to return to my grandparents in the province again. There was no room for us to stay with the elderly. So we went back.

There were only four of us in the house: me, my sibling, my grandfather, and my grandmother. My mother worked in Manila, so she was far from us. I was in Grade 3. That was when the direction of my life began to change.

I made many friends who I knew did not do good things. Delinquents. Children used to trouble. I joined them. That was when I learned about cutting at school. I skipped classes just to wander around. We ran away even when there was class. I joined fights. There were times I even got into real fights.

When my grandmother asked why I had bruises or wounds, I just lied. I said I fell or tripped. Until one day, my grandmother discovered that I had cut myself. When I got home, she hit me. That was the most painful beating I have ever experienced in my life.

My grandfather was the one I spoke to the most. Sometimes I asked him for money for snacks. I also followed him to the fields. Amid those simple days, there was a strange calmness I could not explain.

During that time, my father reappeared after several years. I could still recognize him because I had memories of him from when I was young before he left. My sibling did not recognize him.

He was kind to me, though I did not know who he really was as a person. He wanted to “borrow” me so that my grandparents on his side could see me. He asked my grandmother for permission, and she agreed.

I stayed with them for two or three weeks. Their house was near the sea, so I often bathed in the water. My father’s siblings and grandparents on his side were kind to me. Sometimes I went fishing with my grandfather. He was old, but he still carried me on his back while walking. That is one of the memories that still makes me cry when I remember it.

When my mother found out that my father took me without her permission, she was angry. So she returned me to my grandparents on my mother’s side.

In 2018 or 2019, my father died. It was New Year’s. He was shot by his former boss, as far as I understood as a child. He had changed workplaces, so he was killed.

I saw him lying in the coffin. I was seven or eight years old. I saw my sibling who did not recognize him. My mother came home to see his body. I also saw his new family, the woman who replaced my mother and their child. I saw how much they cried.

His siblings also cried excessively.

But I, not a single tear. I do not know why. I do not know if I felt nothing or was just ashamed to cry.

After the funeral, my mother returned to Manila to work. Whenever she left, I hid to cry alone. Not out of anger, but because I felt something was being lost in my life again.

In 2019, COVID came. At first, my mother could still come home occasionally. She had friends, bus drivers, who quarantined at my grandparents’ house.

One memory I cannot forget is my grandparents fighting out of jealousy. They were old, yet my grandmother still felt jealous of a woman she suspected my grandfather was giving attention to. My grandmother was furious and wanted to kill my grandfather. My mother and her friends were there, so it was very embarrassing.

After a few years, in 2020 or 2021, my mother took us back to Manila. I did not enroll in Grade 4 because we were still using modules. My mother said I would start next year.

From 2021 to 2022, I studied with a module and had a tutor. My cousin, who would become a teacher, taught me basic math and English because I had learned almost nothing in Grade 3.

A few months passed, and my cousin left because of other responsibilities. The year ended.

In Grade 5, face-to-face classes resumed, though masks were still required. I had no self-confidence. I was shy around many people. Even outside the house, when there were crowds, I hid or didn’t proceed.

At school, classmates noticed I did not eat. They teased me for not removing my face mask while eating. I thought it would be easier not to remove it. Even now, I still wear the face mask.

In Grade 5, a new classmate, a girl, transferred in. She was sometimes ill and fainted. I had a crush on her. I was the first person she spoke to in the classroom when she borrowed a ruler. We talked, and I was the only one with her social media back then.

Later, she found other friends and seemed to drift away. I learned she had a boyfriend. I quietly withdrew.

Another girl liked me. She was part of our service group. She confessed to me, and I did not know what to do, so I said yes, though I did not understand what a relationship meant. I was cold to her because I did not really like her.

In another room, another girl liked me. She looked like Jisoo from Blackpink. I ignored her, though I liked her too. I felt I was not suitable for her.

After Grade 5, my mother went abroad to work. That was when I started questioning the meaning of life. If we study and work just to die one day, what is the point? That was when I became an atheist.

In Grade 6, I became addicted to Mobile Legends. I often skipped school due to lack of sleep. I also did household chores because my aunt didn’t care. I cooked and woke up my sibling.

I did not attend graduation because no parent would come.

In Grade 7, school resumed. I made friends: Dar, Kiz, and Dam. But Kiz got into a fight and was hospitalized. Afterwards, he changed sections. I was left alone.

Gradually, I stopped attending until I dropped out entirely. My life became the internet, online games, K-drama, and K-pop. In 2022, I discovered NewJeans and my bias was Hyein, then Hanni.

I also met J on Telegram, a NewJeans fan. We played Mobile Legends together, became close, and even had an affinity rose in the game. It felt like a relationship, yet not really. That lasted three or four months until I decided to end it.

In 2023, I developed Intermittent Explosive Disorder. There were moments I could barely control my anger. I almost hurt my aunt and sibling.

In 2025, we vacationed in the province because my mother was returning from abroad. One day, I got angry at my sibling and almost hit them with a massage metal. That was when I again felt uncontrollable rage.

Back in Manila, I re-enrolled in Grade 7 as a repeater. I told myself I would go just to study, not to make friends.

But I met An, a noisy and chaotic girl who always asked me questions. I also met Jim, Jeeb, Boni, and Dad. They had different religions—Muslim, INC, Christian. I was the only atheist. Gradually, I made friends.

I had a crush I called Chaewon, because she looked like Kim Chaewon from Le Sserafim. But I knew she liked girls, so I did not confess.

That’s when I slowly realized there is joy in being a student. There is joy in having friends.

Now, the school year is almost over. We should be entering Grade 8. But I do not know if I will get there.

I am fifteen years old, but I would only be in Grade 8. I have wasted so much time. I have made so many mistakes.

On the last day of class, while everyone said goodbye to each other, I left the classroom. I did not want to see them because I was crying. That was when I realized that, somehow, life had moments of happiness too. And that is why the decision to disappear is so difficult.

If you read this, I hope you understand that everything I felt was real—the joy, the anger, the confusion, and the search for meaning in a world that sometimes seems without answers.

I am grateful to everyone who has been part of my life, even if at the time it seemed meaningless.

If you see this, I may no longer be here. Dead.

I am still not sure. This is not a final decision.

Maybe tomorrow. Maybe the day after.

I do not know.

This is my farewell.

Dave Christopher
