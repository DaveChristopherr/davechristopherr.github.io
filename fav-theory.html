Kantian Ethics

The Philosophy of Duty and Reason
Why it is my favorite theory

As someone who relies on observation and logic rather than faith, finding a solid foundation for morality can be complex. Immanuel Kant's Ethical Theory stands out to me because it is entirely grounded in pure reason.

"Act only according to that maxim whereby you can, at the same time, will that it should become a universal law."

The core of his philosophy, the Categorical Imperative, suggests that doing the right thing isn't about the promise of a reward or the fear of divine punishment. Instead, it is about recognizing our rational duty. If an action cannot be universally applied to everyone without contradiction, then it is logically and ethically wrong.

Reason Over Emotion

Another profound aspect of Kant's ethics is the idea that we must treat humanity as an end in itself, and never merely as a means to an end. It demands respect for human autonomy. In a world that often feels uncertain and indifferent, choosing to do what is right simply because it is the logical and ethical duty brings a sense of grounded purpose.
